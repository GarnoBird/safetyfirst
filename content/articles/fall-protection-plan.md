# Fall Protection Plan

From BC Construction Safety Wiki

## Summary

A fall protection plan is the site-specific explanation of how a crew will control a fall hazard when the regulation requires a written plan. It should name the hazard, the selected system, the procedures workers must follow, and the rescue method if a worker falls. {{cite:ohsr-11-3}}

The useful version is short enough to brief at the work face and specific enough that a worker can find the anchor, understand the travel limit, and know when to stop. A plan that only says '100 percent tie off' is not enough if it does not explain anchors, clearance, swing-fall risk, equipment inspection, and rescue. {{cite:ohsr-11-6}}

This page supports [[fall-protection|Fall Protection]], [[fall-arrest|Fall Arrest]], and [[fall-rescue-plan|Fall Rescue Plan]]. ## When this applies

- A crew will work where the regulation requires a written fall protection plan.
- Personal fall protection will be used and the setup is not obvious from permanent guardrails or simple access controls.
- A work method changes because rails, covers, anchors, scaffolds, lifts, or roof access have changed.
- The crew needs a rescue method for a possible suspended worker.
- Multiple trades share the same edge, opening, roof area, or elevated platform.
- The plan is being reviewed after an incident, near miss, weather event, or failed inspection.
- A supervisor needs to brief new workers or a replacement crew before elevated work starts.

## Legal requirements

- Prepare a written fall protection plan when Part 11 requires one for the work. {{cite:ohsr-11-3}}
- The plan must identify the fall hazards and describe the fall protection system or systems to be used. {{cite:ohsr-11-3}}
- The plan must describe procedures for assembly, maintenance, inspection, use, and disassembly of the system where those procedures are relevant. {{cite:ohsr-11-3}}
- The plan must describe rescue procedures for a fallen worker when fall arrest is used. {{cite:ohsr-11-3}}
- The selected system still has to meet the underlying Part 11 duty to use fall protection. {{cite:ohsr-11-2}}
- Anchors and equipment referenced by the plan must match the applicable Part 11 requirements. {{cite:ohsr-11-5}} {{cite:ohsr-11-6}}
- Equipment inspections and removal from service must be addressed when the plan depends on personal fall protection equipment. {{cite:ohsr-11-9}}
- Emergency and rescue planning must be checked against the current Part 32 and Part 11 wording before final publication. Reviewer question: confirm this belongs in Legal requirements and is supported by current official BC source material. {{review:source}}

## Best practice

- Put the plan in the language the crew uses, but keep the legal citations tied to the official terms.
- Use a sketch or marked photo for anchors, edges, access, rescue route, and exclusion zones.
- State who can approve field changes and what conditions require work to stop.
- Keep the plan near the work and review it at the toolbox talk before workers enter the exposure.
- Reference manufacturer instructions for harnesses, connectors, self-retracting lifelines, and engineered systems.
- Use a separate lift, swing stage, scaffold, or roof-area plan when the rescue method is different.
- Review the plan after the work sequence changes, not only after an incident.

## Required documents

- Fall protection plan: records the site-specific control expected before the task starts. {{cite:ohsr-11-3}}
- Rescue plan: supports worker instruction, supervisor review, and follow-up when conditions change. {{cite:ohsr-11-2}}
- Site drawing or sketch: keeps the field method tied to the hazard, equipment, and work location. {{cite:ohsr-11-5}}
- Equipment inspection record: helps the prime contractor and affected trades coordinate overlapping work. {{cite:ohsr-11-6}}
- Training/instruction record: creates a review trail if an inspection, incident, or refusal raises the issue. {{cite:ohsr-11-9}}
- Manufacturer instructions: confirms the plan, equipment, or instruction workers are expected to use. {{cite:ohsr-11-10}}

## Step-by-step safe procedure

1. Identify the exact location, elevation, work sequence, crew, equipment, and expected duration.
2. List the fall hazards, including edges, openings, access, swing-fall paths, sharp edges, and lower-level obstructions.
3. Choose the fall protection system and explain why it fits the site condition.
4. Identify anchors, lines, connectors, guardrails, covers, and access routes on a simple sketch or marked photo.
5. Write the rescue method, including equipment, access, contact method, and who starts the response.
6. Confirm the required equipment inspections and removal-from-service triggers. {{cite:ohsr-11-9}}
7. Brief workers before the task starts and confirm they understand the tie-off points and stop-work triggers.
8. Keep the plan available at the work location and update it when conditions change.
9. Coordinate plan changes with the prime contractor and affected trades.
10. Close the task by recording deficiencies, removed equipment, rescue issues, and changes needed before the next shift.

## Worker checklist

- [ ] I have reviewed the fall protection plan for this location.
- [ ] I know the fall hazards the plan is controlling.
- [ ] I know the system I must use and where it starts and ends.
- [ ] I know my approved anchor or barrier.
- [ ] I know what equipment I must inspect before use.
- [ ] I know how rescue would start if a worker fell.
- [ ] I know who can change the plan.
- [ ] I know what condition means the work must stop.
- [ ] I will not improvise a tie-off point or remove a control.
- [ ] I will report if the work face no longer matches the plan.

## Supervisor checklist

- [ ] The written plan matches the actual work area and sequence.
- [ ] The plan covers hazard, system, procedure, inspection, and rescue details. {{cite:ohsr-11-3}}
- [ ] Workers have been briefed before exposure begins.
- [ ] Anchors and equipment identified in the plan are available and suitable for the intended use.
- [ ] The rescue method can be carried out with available access and equipment.
- [ ] The plan is available where workers can use it.
- [ ] Changes are recorded and communicated before work continues.
- [ ] The plan is coordinated with nearby trades and the prime contractor.
- [ ] Deficiencies have owners, interim controls, and follow-up dates.

## Common mistakes

- Using a generic plan that does not name the actual edge, anchor, or rescue method.
- Leaving rescue as 'call 911' when workers could be suspended or unreachable.
- Not updating the plan after guardrails are removed or the work area shifts.
- Listing equipment without checking compatibility or manufacturer instructions.
- Using a plan written for one roof or floor on another area with different hazards.
- Failing to brief replacement workers or late arrivals.
- Treating the plan as office paperwork instead of a field control.
- Not recording who approved a field change.
