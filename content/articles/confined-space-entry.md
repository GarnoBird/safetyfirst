# Confined Space Entry

From BC Construction Safety Wiki

## Summary

Confined space entry is not just working in a tight place. The key issue is whether the space has limited entry or exit and hazards such as poor atmosphere, engulfment, moving equipment, stored energy, or rescue difficulty. {{cite:ohsr-9-5}}

A safe entry depends on the entry program, hazard assessment, isolation, atmospheric testing, ventilation, standby arrangements, communication, permit controls, and rescue service. If rescue is not planned before entry, the entry is not ready. {{cite:ohsr-9-25}} {{cite:ohsr-9-37}}

## When this applies

- Workers may enter a tank, vault, manhole, sump, crawlspace, vessel, pit, duct, excavation connection, or similar space.
- The space may contain hazardous atmosphere, oxygen deficiency, oxygen enrichment, flammable vapours, toxic gases, or dust.
- The space has restricted entry or exit that could slow evacuation or rescue.
- Mechanical, electrical, hydraulic, pneumatic, chemical, or process energy must be isolated.
- Ventilation, atmospheric testing, or a standby person is needed.
- An entry permit is required for the class or condition of the space.
- The work could change the atmosphere through welding, cleaning, coating, pumping, disturbing sludge, or using combustion equipment.

## Legal requirements

- Establish and follow a confined space entry program where the regulation requires one. {{cite:ohsr-9-5}}
- Use an entry permit when the regulation requires a permit for the space and work. {{cite:ohsr-9-13}}
- Include required permit information such as space identification, workers, hazards, controls, tests, and authorization. {{cite:ohsr-9-14}}
- Verify required precautions before workers enter the space. {{cite:ohsr-9-24}}
- Test the atmosphere as required before and during entry. {{cite:ohsr-9-25}}
- Provide a standby person where required by the confined space classification and controls. {{cite:ohsr-9-34}}
- Provide rescue services before entry where required. {{cite:ohsr-9-37}}
- Confirm classification, testing frequency, standby level, and rescue requirements against the current regulation before source-checked status. Reviewer question: confirm this belongs in Legal requirements and is supported by current official BC source material. {{review:source}}

## Best practice

- Treat every entry as a system: permit, isolation, testing, ventilation, communication, standby, and rescue.
- Use continuous monitoring when the work or space can change the atmosphere.
- Keep the attendant focused on the entry; do not assign production work that pulls them away.
- Use physical isolation and lockout before relying on signs or verbal controls.
- Ventilate before entry where appropriate and keep ventilation equipment protected from exhaust sources.
- Practice rescue communication and access before entry starts.
- Stop entry when the work changes from the permit conditions.

## Required documents

- Confined space entry program: records the site-specific control expected before the task starts. {{cite:ohsr-9-5}}
- Entry permit: supports worker instruction, supervisor review, and follow-up when conditions change. {{cite:ohsr-9-13}}
- Hazard assessment: keeps the field method tied to the hazard, equipment, and work location. {{cite:ohsr-9-14}}
- Atmospheric test record: helps the prime contractor and affected trades coordinate overlapping work. {{cite:ohsr-9-24}}
- Rescue plan: creates a review trail if an inspection, incident, or refusal raises the issue. {{cite:ohsr-9-25}}
- Lockout/isolation record: confirms the plan, equipment, or instruction workers are expected to use. {{cite:ohsr-9-34}}

## Step-by-step safe procedure

1. Identify the space and decide whether it is a confined space under the site program.
2. Complete or review the hazard assessment, including atmosphere, engulfment, energy, access, and rescue.
3. Isolate hazardous energy, materials, lines, equipment, and moving parts before entry.
4. Set up ventilation, lighting, communication, access, retrieval, and rescue equipment.
5. Test the atmosphere in the required order and locations before entry. {{cite:ohsr-9-25}}
6. Complete the permit when required and brief entrants, standby person, supervisor, and rescue contacts.
7. Control access so only authorized entrants enter.
8. Monitor the atmosphere and conditions during the work.
9. Stop entry if alarms sound, ventilation fails, the work changes, communication is lost, or a worker feels unwell.
10. Close the permit, account for workers, remove equipment, and document changes before re-entry.

## Worker checklist

- [ ] I know why this space is treated as a confined space.
- [ ] I am listed or authorized for the entry.
- [ ] I know the hazards and controls on the permit.
- [ ] I know the atmospheric test results and alarm instructions.
- [ ] I know the communication method with the standby person.
- [ ] I know how to leave the space quickly.
- [ ] I will not enter if the permit, testing, ventilation, standby, or rescue plan is missing.
- [ ] I will leave immediately if conditions change or I feel unwell.
- [ ] I will not bring unapproved chemicals, tools, or combustion equipment into the space.
- [ ] I know rescue starts from outside the space unless the rescue plan says otherwise.

## Supervisor checklist

- [ ] The confined space entry program applies to this space and task. {{cite:ohsr-9-5}}
- [ ] Permit requirements are met before entry where a permit is required. {{cite:ohsr-9-13}}
- [ ] Isolation, testing, ventilation, access, communication, and rescue are verified before entry. {{cite:ohsr-9-24}}
- [ ] Atmospheric testing is done by assigned competent personnel using suitable equipment.
- [ ] Entrants and standby persons understand their duties.
- [ ] Rescue services are available before entry begins. {{cite:ohsr-9-37}}
- [ ] Changing work conditions trigger a permit pause and reassessment.
- [ ] The prime contractor and affected employers are coordinated when multiple crews are involved.
- [ ] Records are kept for permits, tests, training, and deficiencies.

## Common mistakes

- Treating a short entry as safe because the worker will only be inside for a minute.
- Testing only at the opening and not where the worker will breathe.
- Using a standby person who is also doing unrelated work.
- Assuming ventilation is working without checking airflow and exhaust location.
- Entering to rescue a worker without the planned rescue method.
- Forgetting lockout, blanking, draining, or isolation before entry.
- Using combustion equipment or coatings without reassessing the atmosphere.
- Letting the permit stay open after the work condition changes.
