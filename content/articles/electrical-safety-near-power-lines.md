# Electrical Safety Near Power Lines

From BC Construction Safety Wiki

## Summary

Power-line work is high risk because workers and equipment can be injured without touching the conductor directly. Cranes, pump booms, ladders, scaffolds, dump boxes, rebar, roofing materials, and long tools can enter an unsafe distance quickly. {{cite:ohsr-19-24-1}}

The field control starts by identifying overhead and nearby high-voltage conductors, establishing limits, getting required assurances or authorizations, and keeping mobile equipment, loads, and workers out of the danger zone. {{cite:ohsr-19-25}}

Use this article with [[cranes-near-power-lines|Cranes Near Power Lines]], [[cranes-and-hoists|Cranes and Hoists]], [[mobile-equipment|Mobile Equipment]], and [[temporary-power|Temporary Power]]. This is a Tier 1 draft pending source review. {{review:source}}

## When this applies

- A crane, boom truck, pump truck, telehandler, scaffold, ladder, hoist, lift, or long material will be used near overhead lines.
- Workers are setting up forms, roofing, siding, rebar, signage, hoarding, or equipment near energized conductors.
- The site has temporary power, distribution lines, trolley lines, service drops, or nearby utility poles.
- A worker cannot clearly identify the voltage, ownership, or required clearance.
- The work area changes because equipment moves, boom radius changes, or loads swing.
- Utility owner authorization, written assurance, or de-energization may be needed.
- An incident response plan is needed for electrical contact, step potential, or equipment energization.

## Legal requirements

- Maintain the required minimum distance from high-voltage conductors unless an allowed control or authorization is in place. {{cite:ohsr-19-24-1}}
- Obtain written assurance where required before work near high-voltage conductors proceeds. {{cite:ohsr-19-25}}
- Obtain authorization by the owner where required for work near energized equipment. {{cite:ohsr-19-29}}
- Coordinate crane setup and operation with crane and hoist standards when hoisting equipment is part of the exposure. {{cite:ohsr-14-2}}
- Use clear signalling and communication where equipment movement could approach the hazard. {{cite:ohsr-15-20}}
- Use de-energization and lockout principles where equipment or circuits are intentionally isolated for work. {{cite:ohsr-10-6}}
- Confirm current WorkSafeBC and utility-owner requirements before using distance tables in public copy. {{review:source}}
- Confirm emergency work exceptions and utility-owner procedures before field publication. {{review:source}}

## Best practice

- Mark power lines on the site map, lift plan, orientation, and delivery route.
- Use physical barriers, goal posts, spotters, signage, and equipment limits where needed.
- Keep workers away from equipment if contact occurs until the emergency plan says it is safe.
- Do not rely on visual judgement from an operator alone when booms or loads can swing.
- Coordinate with the utility owner early if de-energizing, insulating, relocating, or written assurance may be needed.
- Review power-line controls during lift planning and daily coordination meetings.
- Use the emergency plan to explain step potential and who calls the utility.

## Required documents

- Power-line risk assessment: records the site-specific control expected before the task starts. {{cite:ohsr-19-24-1}}
- Written assurance or authorization: supports worker instruction, supervisor review, and follow-up when conditions change. {{cite:ohsr-19-25}}
- Work procedure: keeps the field method tied to the hazard, equipment, and work location. {{cite:ohsr-19-29}}
- Traffic/equipment control plan: helps the prime contractor and affected trades coordinate overlapping work. {{cite:ohsr-14-2}}
- Emergency response plan: creates a review trail if an inspection, incident, or refusal raises the issue. {{cite:ohsr-15-20}}
- Training/instruction record: confirms the plan, equipment, or instruction workers are expected to use. {{cite:ohsr-10-6}}

## Step-by-step safe procedure

1. Identify all overhead and nearby electrical conductors before equipment is set up.
2. Confirm voltage, ownership, minimum distance, and whether written assurance or authorization is required.
3. Plan work to stay outside restricted distances by changing location, sequence, equipment, or method where practical.
4. Contact the utility owner or qualified party when de-energization, relocation, barriers, or written assurance may be needed.
5. Set physical limits, signage, spotters, and exclusion zones before equipment moves.
6. Brief workers on approach limits, communication, emergency response, and stop-work triggers.
7. Monitor boom, load, line, scaffold, ladder, and material position during the task.
8. Stop work if clearance cannot be verified or weather, load swing, or equipment movement changes the exposure.
9. If contact occurs, follow the emergency plan, keep others away, and summon utility/emergency help.
10. Record approvals, briefings, deficiencies, and changes to the work method.

## Worker checklist

- [ ] I know where overhead or nearby power lines are.
- [ ] I know the work limit or exclusion zone for this task.
- [ ] I will not move ladders, pipe, rebar, scaffold parts, or equipment near lines without direction.
- [ ] I know who is spotting or controlling equipment movement.
- [ ] I will stop if a boom, load, or material could enter the restricted area.
- [ ] I will not approach energized equipment after contact unless told it is safe by the emergency plan.
- [ ] I know how to call the supervisor if I see a line hazard.
- [ ] I know weather and load swing can change clearance.
- [ ] I will keep other workers and the public out of the hazard area.
- [ ] I will ask if I cannot identify the line or required distance.

## Supervisor checklist

- [ ] Power lines and electrical conductors are identified before work starts.
- [ ] Minimum distance, written assurance, and authorization needs are confirmed. {{cite:ohsr-19-24-1}} {{cite:ohsr-19-25}}
- [ ] Equipment, load paths, scaffold locations, and material handling routes avoid the restricted area.
- [ ] Spotters, barriers, signage, and physical limits are used where needed.
- [ ] Workers understand emergency response for contact and step potential.
- [ ] Utility owner coordination is complete where required.
- [ ] Crane, rigging, and delivery plans address power-line exposure.
- [ ] Controls are rechecked when equipment moves or conditions change.
- [ ] Records are kept for authorization, briefing, and deficiencies.
- [ ] Distance and authorization wording is source checked before public-ready status. {{review:source}}

## Common mistakes

- Planning the lift first and checking power lines after the crane arrives.
- Assuming insulated-looking lines are safe to approach.
- Using a spotter with no authority to stop the equipment.
- Letting long materials swing or be carried upright near lines.
- Forgetting service drops and temporary power lines.
- Walking toward energized equipment after contact.
- Relying on memory instead of utility confirmation for voltage and ownership.
- Ignoring wind, boom deflection, load swing, and changing radius.
