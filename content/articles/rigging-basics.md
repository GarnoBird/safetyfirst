# Rigging Basics

From BC Construction Safety Wiki

## Summary

Rigging is the connection between the load and the crane, hoist, forklift attachment, or lifting device. If the wrong sling, shackle, hook, hitch, angle, edge protection, or lifting point is used, the load can shift, fall, or crush workers during landing. {{cite:ohsr-15-4}}

Basic rigging work still requires competent judgement. A worker needs to know the load weight, center of gravity, sling angle, condition of rigging, communication method, and where people will stand before the lift starts. {{cite:ohsr-15-2}}

Use this article with [[cranes-and-hoists|Cranes and Hoists]], [[lift-plans|Lift Plans]], and [[material-handling|Material Handling]].

## When this applies

- A worker selects, inspects, connects, adjusts, lands, or removes slings, shackles, hooks, chokers, spreader bars, or lifting points.
- A load will be lifted by crane, hoist, boom truck, forklift attachment, telehandler, or other equipment.
- The load is awkward, unbalanced, sharp-edged, fragile, bundled, long, or not marked with weight.
- A lift requires tag lines, special hitches, multiple slings, or edge protection.
- Workers are near pinch points while the load is landed or unhooked.
- Rigging has damaged tags, cuts, deformation, heat damage, chemical damage, or unknown history.
- The lift requires hand signals or radio communication.

## Legal requirements

- Use qualified riggers where Part 15 requires rigging to be done by qualified workers. {{cite:ohsr-15-2}}
- Detach loads safely and only when the load is stable and supported as required. {{cite:ohsr-15-3}}
- Use rigging in accordance with the regulation, its intended purpose, and applicable instructions. {{cite:ohsr-15-4}}
- Use recognized hand signals or communication methods for crane work. {{cite:ohsr-15-20}}
- Inspect slings before use and remove defective rigging from service. {{cite:ohsr-15-31}}
- Coordinate rigging requirements with the crane and hoist standards that apply to the lifting equipment. {{cite:ohsr-14-2}}

## Best practice

- Use a short pre-lift talk for unusual loads, blind lifts, multiple riggers, or congested landing areas.
- Keep rigging protected from sharp concrete, steel edges, heat, chemicals, and crushing.
- Use tag lines only from a safe position with an escape path.
- Never put hands under a load or between the load and structure during landing.
- Confirm the load is stable before slackening or removing rigging.
- Store rigging off the ground, dry, and away from damage paths.
- Use damaged-rigging quarantine so defective gear does not return to service.

## Required documents

- Rigging inspection record: records the site-specific control expected before the task starts. {{cite:ohsr-15-2}}
- Lift plan: supports worker instruction, supervisor review, and follow-up when conditions change. {{cite:ohsr-15-3}}
- Manufacturer instructions: keeps the field method tied to the hazard, equipment, and work location. {{cite:ohsr-15-4}}
- Training/competency record: helps the prime contractor and affected trades coordinate overlapping work. {{cite:ohsr-15-20}}
- Load weight information: creates a review trail if an inspection, incident, or refusal raises the issue. {{cite:ohsr-15-31}}
- Deficiency log: confirms the plan, equipment, or instruction workers are expected to use. {{cite:ohsr-14-2}}

## Step-by-step safe procedure

1. Identify the load, weight, center of gravity, lift points, sharp edges, and landing method.
2. Select rigging with suitable capacity, hitch, length, angle, and compatibility.
3. Inspect slings, shackles, hooks, latches, pins, tags, stitching, links, and hardware before use.
4. Protect rigging from sharp edges, heat, chemicals, and abrasion.
5. Confirm the signal person, communication method, and exclusion zone.
6. Connect rigging so the load lifts level or as planned.
7. Lift a few inches and check balance, security, clearances, and worker position.
8. Keep clear of suspended loads and pinch points during travel and landing.
9. Detach only after the load is stable and supported. {{cite:ohsr-15-3}}
10. Remove damaged, overloaded, or uncertain rigging from service and record the deficiency.

## Worker checklist

- [ ] I know the load weight or who confirmed it.
- [ ] I inspected the rigging before use.
- [ ] I know the hitch and connection method for this load.
- [ ] I checked for sharp edges and pinch points.
- [ ] I know who is signalling the lift.
- [ ] I will not stand under the load.
- [ ] I will keep hands out from under and between the load.
- [ ] I will not use damaged, untagged, or uncertain rigging.
- [ ] I will not remove rigging until the load is stable.
- [ ] I will stop the lift if communication, balance, or worker position is unsafe.

## Supervisor checklist

- [ ] The rigger is qualified for the assigned rigging work. {{cite:ohsr-15-2}}
- [ ] The load weight, center of gravity, hitch, sling angle, and landing method are understood.
- [ ] Rigging is inspected before use. {{cite:ohsr-15-31}}
- [ ] Damaged rigging is removed from service and cannot be reused by mistake.
- [ ] Signal person, communication method, and exclusion zone are in place.
- [ ] The rigging method matches the lift plan and equipment.
- [ ] Workers are protected from suspended loads and landing pinch points.
- [ ] Tag lines, if used, can be handled from a safe position.
- [ ] Lift changes trigger a pause and review.

## Common mistakes

- Using a sling because it is nearby instead of because it is rated and suitable.
- Ignoring sling angle and reduced capacity.
- Choking sharp material without edge protection.
- Putting hands under the load to pull rigging out.
- Using untagged or damaged rigging for one more lift.
- Letting workers stand in the fall zone while the load is adjusted.
- Removing rigging before the load is stable.
- Using unclear signals or multiple signal persons.
