# Occupational First Aid Requirements

From BC Construction Safety Wiki

## Summary

Occupational first aid requirements tell the employer what first aid resources, attendants, procedures, communication, supplies, facilities, records, and transportation arrangements are needed for the workplace. On construction sites, the answer can change with crew size, location, access, remoteness, and hazard level. {{cite:ohsr-3-16}}

The useful field test is whether a worker can get first aid quickly, the attendant can be contacted, supplies are available, and transportation has been planned before a serious incident happens. {{cite:ohsr-3-18}}

Use this article with [[first-aid-assessment|First Aid Assessment]], [[first-aid-room|First Aid Room]], [[cardiac-arrest-on-site|Cardiac Arrest on Site]], and [[site-emergency-response-plan|Site Emergency Response Plan]]. This is a Tier 1 draft pending source review. {{review:source}}

## When this applies

- A construction site is being set up, expanded, reduced, moved, or reviewed.
- Crew size, work location, travel time, hazard level, or shift coverage changes.
- The site needs attendants, kits, rooms, emergency communication, or transportation arrangements.
- A serious injury, near miss, or first aid delay shows the current setup may be inadequate.
- Work is remote, high-rise, underground, in traffic, or hard for ambulances to access.
- A first aid record, attendant certificate, supply inspection, or procedure is missing.
- Workers do not know how to summon first aid.

## Legal requirements

- Provide occupational first aid resources based on the basic requirements in Part 3. {{cite:ohsr-3-16}}
- Develop and communicate first aid procedures for the workplace. {{cite:ohsr-3-17}}
- Make first aid communication and availability arrangements effective for the workplace. {{cite:ohsr-3-18}}
- Maintain first aid records as required while protecting worker privacy. {{cite:ohsr-3-19}}
- Ensure first aid attendants carry out their assigned responsibilities within the regulation. {{cite:ohsr-3-21}}
- Coordinate emergency rescue and evacuation worker safety where site emergency response requires it. {{cite:ohsr-32-4}}
- Confirm current WorkSafeBC first aid assessment tables and amendments before public-ready publication. {{review:source}}
- Do not use this article as medical advice or as a substitute for certified first aid training. {{review:source}}

## Best practice

- Post first aid contact information where workers actually enter, gather, and call from.
- Check first aid access at height, underground, in parkades, and behind locked gates.
- Plan ambulance routing, elevator use, crane/loading conflicts, and gate access before an emergency.
- Keep first aid records private and separate from general toolbox or production notes.
- Reassess after crew growth, night shifts, remote work, high-risk tasks, or site layout changes.
- Use drills or table-top reviews for cardiac arrest, falls, crush injuries, and confined space incidents.
- Link first aid planning to emergency transportation, AED access, and serious incident reporting.

## Required documents

- First aid assessment: records the site-specific control expected before the task starts. {{cite:ohsr-3-16}}
- First aid procedures: supports worker instruction, supervisor review, and follow-up when conditions change. {{cite:ohsr-3-17}}
- First aid record: keeps the field method tied to the hazard, equipment, and work location. {{cite:ohsr-3-18}}
- Emergency transportation plan: helps the prime contractor and affected trades coordinate overlapping work. {{cite:ohsr-3-19}}
- Training/certification record: creates a review trail if an inspection, incident, or refusal raises the issue. {{cite:ohsr-3-21}}
- Inspection record: confirms the plan, equipment, or instruction workers are expected to use. {{cite:ohsr-32-4}}

## Step-by-step safe procedure

1. Complete or review the workplace first aid assessment.
2. Confirm attendants, supplies, facilities, communication, and transportation match the assessment.
3. Post first aid procedures and contact paths in visible locations.
4. Brief workers during orientation and toolbox talks on how to summon first aid.
5. Check first aid supplies, AED location if available, and access routes regularly.
6. Confirm attendants are available for the shift, site area, and work activity.
7. Protect privacy when recording first aid treatment.
8. Review arrangements after an incident, near miss, first aid delay, or site change.
9. Coordinate emergency access with the prime contractor, gate control, hoist/elevator plan, and traffic control.
10. Document deficiencies and update the assessment when conditions change.

## Worker checklist

- [ ] I know how to call first aid on this site.
- [ ] I know where the first aid supplies or room are.
- [ ] I know who the first aid attendant is or how to find them.
- [ ] I know the emergency muster or access point if directed there.
- [ ] I will report injuries promptly.
- [ ] I will not record private medical details in public site documents.
- [ ] I know where the AED is if the site has one.
- [ ] I know how emergency vehicles reach this work area.
- [ ] I will keep first aid routes and access clear.
- [ ] I will tell the supervisor if posted first aid information is missing or outdated.

## Supervisor checklist

- [ ] The first aid assessment is current for crew size, hazards, distance, and shift coverage. {{cite:ohsr-3-16}}
- [ ] First aid procedures are written and communicated. {{cite:ohsr-3-17}}
- [ ] Communication with first aid is effective from the actual work areas. {{cite:ohsr-3-18}}
- [ ] First aid records are handled with privacy controls. {{cite:ohsr-3-19}}
- [ ] Attendants, supplies, facilities, and transportation match the assessment.
- [ ] Workers know how to summon help.
- [ ] Emergency access, gate control, hoists, and traffic plans support transport.
- [ ] Deficiencies in supplies, posting, rooms, or attendant coverage are corrected promptly.
- [ ] The plan is reviewed after incident or site changes.
- [ ] Current first aid amendments and assessment tables are source checked before public-ready maturity. {{review:source}}

## Common mistakes

- Using last month's crew count after the site has grown.
- Posting first aid information only in the trailer when crews work on upper floors or remote areas.
- Forgetting transport planning for high-rise, underground, or locked-gate areas.
- Treating first aid records like general incident notes instead of private records.
- Not checking shift coverage for overtime or weekend work.
- Letting first aid supplies run low without inspection.
- Assuming an AED exists or is accessible without checking location and access.
- Failing to review first aid setup after a serious incident or first aid delay.
