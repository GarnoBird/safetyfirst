# Confined Space Rescue

From BC Construction Safety Wiki

## Summary

Confined space rescue has to be planned before entry because a delayed or improvised rescue can injure both the entrant and the would-be rescuer. The rescue method must match the space, atmosphere, access, work, and available people or rescue service. {{cite:ohsr-9-37}}

Calling emergency services may be part of the plan, but it is not the whole plan unless the site has confirmed the service can respond, access the space, and perform the required rescue in time for the hazard. {{cite:ohsr-9-39}} {{cite:ohsr-9-40}}

Use this article with [[confined-space-entry|Confined Space Entry]], [[site-emergency-response-plan|Site Emergency Response Plan]], and [[occupational-first-aid-requirements|Occupational First Aid Requirements]]. This is a Tier 1 draft pending source review. {{review:source}}

## When this applies

- A worker could enter a confined space and become unable to exit without assistance.
- The space has atmospheric, engulfment, mechanical, access, vertical-entry, or medical rescue hazards.
- A standby person, attendant, retrieval line, tripod, winch, rescue team, or emergency service is part of the entry plan.
- The entry permit needs rescue arrangements confirmed before authorization.
- The site is remote, congested, underground, in traffic, or otherwise hard for emergency services to access.
- Workers are relying on non-entry rescue and retrieval equipment.
- The rescue plan has not been drilled or has not been matched to the actual opening and route.

## Legal requirements

- Provide rescue services for confined space entry where required by Part 9. {{cite:ohsr-9-37}}
- Make rescue notification arrangements that match the required rescue service. {{cite:ohsr-9-39}}
- Have an effective method to summon rescue when needed. {{cite:ohsr-9-40}}
- Provide a standby person where the regulation requires one for the space and entry condition. {{cite:ohsr-9-34}}
- Use atmospheric testing information to decide whether rescue personnel could be exposed to the same hazard. {{cite:ohsr-9-25}}
- Tie rescue planning to the confined space entry program. {{cite:ohsr-9-5}}
- Account for emergency rescue and evacuation worker safety in the site emergency plan. {{cite:ohsr-32-4}}
- Confirm rescue-service capability, drill frequency, and equipment requirements against current official sources. {{review:source}}

## Best practice

- Prefer non-entry rescue when it can be set up without creating new hazards.
- Confirm the rescue route with the actual equipment, not just on paper.
- Keep rescue equipment at the entry location and protect it from traffic, weather, and damage.
- Make the standby person's only job the entry, communication, and emergency response role.
- Do a short rescue walk-through before entry starts.
- Confirm first aid, transport, and access for emergency responders.
- Stop entry if rescue availability changes.

## Required documents

- Confined space rescue plan: records the site-specific control expected before the task starts. {{cite:ohsr-9-37}}
- Entry permit: supports worker instruction, supervisor review, and follow-up when conditions change. {{cite:ohsr-9-39}}
- Atmospheric test record: keeps the field method tied to the hazard, equipment, and work location. {{cite:ohsr-9-40}}
- Rescue equipment inspection: helps the prime contractor and affected trades coordinate overlapping work. {{cite:ohsr-9-34}}
- Training/drill record: creates a review trail if an inspection, incident, or refusal raises the issue. {{cite:ohsr-9-25}}
- Emergency contact plan: confirms the plan, equipment, or instruction workers are expected to use. {{cite:ohsr-9-5}}

## Step-by-step safe procedure

1. Review the confined space hazard assessment and entry permit.
2. Choose the rescue method for the specific space, opening, depth, atmosphere, and worker position.
3. Confirm whether rescue will be non-entry, entry rescue by trained rescuers, or external rescue service support.
4. Set up retrieval, tripod, winch, harness, communication, lighting, access, and traffic control as needed.
5. Confirm atmospheric testing and ventilation before and during entry.
6. Brief entrants, standby person, supervisor, first aid, and rescue contacts on roles and triggers.
7. Keep rescue equipment immediately available and unobstructed.
8. Stop entry if communication, rescue equipment, standby coverage, or rescue service availability changes.
9. If an emergency occurs, summon rescue using the planned method and keep untrained workers out of the space.
10. Document the rescue readiness check, drills, deficiencies, and changes before the next entry.

## Worker checklist

- [ ] I know the rescue method before I enter.
- [ ] I know who the standby person is.
- [ ] I know the communication method and emergency signal.
- [ ] I know whether retrieval equipment is being used.
- [ ] I will not enter if rescue arrangements are missing or unclear.
- [ ] I will leave if communication or monitoring is lost.
- [ ] I will not attempt an unplanned entry rescue.
- [ ] I know where first aid and emergency access are.
- [ ] I know what condition means the permit is no longer valid.
- [ ] I will report rescue equipment problems before entry continues.

## Supervisor checklist

- [ ] Rescue services are confirmed before entry. {{cite:ohsr-9-37}}
- [ ] The rescue notification method works from the entry location. {{cite:ohsr-9-40}}
- [ ] The standby person understands duties and is not distracted by other work.
- [ ] Rescue equipment matches the space and is available at the entry point.
- [ ] Emergency access, first aid, and transport have been checked.
- [ ] Atmospheric hazards are considered for rescuers as well as entrants.
- [ ] The crew understands that unplanned entry rescue is not allowed.
- [ ] Rescue changes trigger permit suspension and reassessment.
- [ ] Training and drill records are maintained where required by the program.
- [ ] Rescue assumptions are source checked before public-ready maturity. {{review:source}}

## Common mistakes

- Writing 'call 911' without checking access, response, and rescue capability.
- Leaving rescue equipment in a truck instead of at the entry location.
- Letting the standby person help with production work.
- Using retrieval equipment that does not fit the opening or worker position.
- Forgetting that rescuers can be exposed to the same atmosphere.
- Not testing communication from inside the space.
- Continuing entry after rescue service availability changes.
- Allowing coworkers to enter in panic without the planned rescue controls.
