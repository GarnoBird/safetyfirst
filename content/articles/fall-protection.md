# Fall Protection

From BC Construction Safety Wiki

## Summary

Fall protection starts with choosing the control that actually prevents a worker from falling, not with handing out a harness. In BC construction, the basic question is whether the work exposes a worker to a fall hazard and whether the site can use [[guardrails|guardrails]], [[fall-restraint|fall restraint]], [[fall-arrest|fall arrest]], another equivalent system, or a work method that removes the exposure. {{cite:ohsr-11-2}}

A supervisor should be able to point to the fall hazard, the chosen control, the anchor or barrier, and the rescue method before the crew starts. If the job relies on personal fall protection, the setup has to match the equipment instructions, anchor requirements, inspection requirements, and the site condition, including swing-fall paths and sharp edges. {{cite:ohsr-11-6}}

## When this applies

- A worker could fall from an unguarded edge, roof, work platform, slab, scaffold, ladder access point, or temporary opening.
- Guardrails, covers, restraint lines, arrest gear, rope access, swing stages, or other fall protection systems are being selected or changed.
- The work is near a leading edge, elevator shaft, stair opening, formwork edge, excavation edge, or incomplete floor area.
- A crew member is using a harness, lanyard, lifeline, self-retracting lifeline, anchor, horizontal lifeline, or travel restraint system.
- Weather, lighting, housekeeping, material storage, or other trades could change the fall exposure during the shift.
- A rescue method is needed because a worker could be suspended after a fall arrest.
- A field inspection finds missing rails, loose covers, damaged gear, unknown anchors, or workers tied to the wrong point.

## Legal requirements

- Use a fall protection system when the regulation requires it for the work condition, including the height and surface conditions described by Part 11. {{cite:ohsr-11-2}}
- Prepare a written fall protection plan when required by Part 11, including the hazards, system, procedures, and rescue method for the work. {{cite:ohsr-11-3}}
- Select body support equipment that is appropriate for the work and does not create avoidable injury risk during fall arrest. {{cite:ohsr-11-4}}
- Use fall protection equipment that meets the applicable standard or requirement for the component and task. {{cite:ohsr-11-5}}
- Confirm anchor points are adequate for the intended fall protection system before workers connect to them. {{cite:ohsr-11-6}}
- Inspect and maintain fall protection equipment so damaged, worn, altered, or suspect equipment is not used. {{cite:ohsr-11-9}}
- Remove fall protection equipment from service after a fall arrest or when damage, defects, or uncertainty make it unreliable. {{cite:ohsr-11-10}}

## Best practice

- Use guardrails or equivalent edge protection before personal fall protection when the work area can be protected that way.
- Mark every anchor that workers are expected to use, and keep workers away from anchors that are temporary, unknown, or not approved for the load.
- Walk the fall path before work starts and look for swing-fall hazards, lower-level contact, sharp edges, and material that could cut a lifeline.
- Keep the rescue plan practical: name the equipment, access route, contact method, and person who starts rescue.
- Re-brief the crew after wind, rain, material deliveries, guardrail changes, floor openings, or crew changes.
- Use [[fall-rescue-plan|fall rescue planning]] as part of the fall protection setup, not as a separate paperwork exercise.
- Document the inspection of unusual anchors, temporary systems, horizontal lifelines, and any equipment removed from service.

## Required documents

- Fall protection plan: records the site-specific control expected before the task starts. {{cite:ohsr-11-2}}
- Rescue plan: supports worker instruction, supervisor review, and follow-up when conditions change. {{cite:ohsr-11-3}}
- Equipment inspection record: keeps the field method tied to the hazard, equipment, and work location. {{cite:ohsr-11-4}}
- Training/instruction record: helps the prime contractor and affected trades coordinate overlapping work. {{cite:ohsr-11-5}}
- Manufacturer instructions: creates a review trail if an inspection, incident, or refusal raises the issue. {{cite:ohsr-11-6}}
- Site-specific safe work procedure: confirms the plan, equipment, or instruction workers are expected to use. {{cite:ohsr-11-9}}

## Step-by-step safe procedure

1. Walk the work area and identify every edge, opening, access point, lower level, and changing work face.
2. Choose the fall protection method in this order where practicable: eliminate the exposure, guard it, restrain the worker, then use arrest only when stronger controls are not practical.
3. If personal fall protection is used, identify each anchor, connector, body support, lifeline, clearance limit, and swing-fall path.
4. Check the written fall protection plan if one is required, and make sure it matches today's location and sequence. {{cite:ohsr-11-3}}
5. Inspect harnesses, lanyards, lifelines, self-retracting lifelines, hooks, labels, and storage condition before use. {{cite:ohsr-11-9}}
6. Confirm rescue access before work starts, including how the crew will reach a suspended worker and how first aid will be contacted.
7. Brief workers on tie-off points, travel limits, rescue, stop-work triggers, and who can approve field changes.
8. Install, verify, and protect guardrails, covers, anchors, and access before production work begins.
9. Stop work if a guardrail is removed, an opening is uncovered, equipment is damaged, an anchor is uncertain, or weather changes the exposure.
10. Record deficiencies, removed equipment, plan changes, and corrective actions before the next shift continues the work.

## Worker checklist

- [ ] I know where the fall hazard is for my task today.
- [ ] I know whether I am protected by guardrails, restraint, arrest, or another control.
- [ ] I inspected my fall protection equipment before using it.
- [ ] I know my approved anchor or the barrier I must stay behind.
- [ ] I know my travel limits and what could create a swing fall.
- [ ] I know how rescue would start if someone fell.
- [ ] I will not move a guardrail, cover, anchor, or lifeline without supervisor approval.
- [ ] I will stop if equipment is damaged, missing a label, altered, or has arrested a fall.
- [ ] I will report uncovered openings, loose covers, incomplete rails, or workers tied to the wrong point.
- [ ] I will ask before continuing if the work no longer matches the plan.

## Supervisor checklist

- [ ] The fall hazard assessment matches today's task, location, and work sequence.
- [ ] The chosen control is installed before workers are exposed.
- [ ] A fall protection plan is available where Part 11 requires one. {{cite:ohsr-11-3}}
- [ ] Workers have been instructed on the specific system, anchors, travel limits, and rescue method.
- [ ] Anchors, lifelines, guardrails, covers, and access points have been checked in the field.
- [ ] Damaged or suspect equipment has been removed from service. {{cite:ohsr-11-10}}
- [ ] The rescue method can be started quickly with available people, access, and equipment.
- [ ] Adjacent trades, material deliveries, weather, and changing floor openings are controlled.
- [ ] Fall protection deficiencies are recorded with an owner, interim control, and follow-up time.

## Common mistakes

- Treating a harness as the plan when the anchor, clearance, travel path, and rescue method have not been checked.
- Using fall arrest where fall restraint or guardrails could remove the exposure.
- Leaving covers or rails for another trade to fix after workers have already entered the area.
- Assuming a structural member is an approved anchor without review.
- Ignoring swing fall and lower-level contact when selecting a tie-off point.
- Not removing equipment from service after a fall arrest or visible damage.
- Writing a fall protection plan once and reusing it after the work face changes.
- Relying on emergency responders for rescue without a site method to reach the worker.
